# Stage 1: Problem Validation Reference

---

## Core Interview Principle

**Never ask opinion questions. Only ask about the past.**

The same principle applies whether you're talking to a consumer or a CFO: people can't reliably predict their own future behavior, but they can accurately describe what they've already done.

**WRONG (opinion):**
- "Would you use this?"
- "Do you think this is a good idea?"
- "Would you pay €X for this?"

**RIGHT (behavior):**
- "Tell me about the last time you dealt with this problem."
- "How do you currently handle [situation]?"
- "How much time/money does that cost you today?"
- "What have you tried to solve this? What happened?"
- "Who else deals with this — can you introduce me?"

**B2C framing — Customer Discovery Interview (Mom Test):** Ask questions your mom couldn't lie to make you feel better about. She'll always say your idea is great — but she can't lie about her own behavior.

**B2B framing — Stakeholder Discovery Interview:** You're talking to an economic buyer or decision-maker, not a friend. Their job depends on making the right call. Focus on cost quantification, org commitment signals, and buying process — not emotional resonance.

## Interview Script Template (B2C — Customer Discovery)

```
[Opening — 2 min]
"Thanks for taking the time. I'm researching [problem area]. I'm not here to sell anything
— I just want to understand how people like you deal with [situation]. Okay if I ask you
about your experience?"

[Current behavior — 5-8 min]
"Can you walk me through the last time you dealt with [problem]?"
"How often does this come up for you?"
"What do you do when it happens?"
"How much time/money does your current approach cost?"

[Probing — 5-8 min]
"What's the most frustrating part of that?"
"Have you ever tried to find a better solution? What happened?"
"What would the ideal situation look like?"

[Closing — 2 min]
"Is there anyone else I should talk to about this?"
"Would you be open to following up if I have more questions?"
```

## Interview Script Template (B2B — Stakeholder Discovery)

```
[Opening — 3 min]
"Thanks for meeting with me. I'm researching [problem area] in [industry]. I'm trying to
understand how [role] at companies like yours deal with [situation]. This is purely
exploratory — no sales pitch today. Can I ask you about your current process?"

[Current state — 10-15 min]
"Walk me through how your team currently handles [process/situation]."
"What does that cost you in time or money? Can you put a number on it?"
"What systems/tools are you using today for this?"
"Who else is involved in this process?"

[Pain probing — 10-15 min]
"What's the biggest frustration with your current approach?"
"Have you ever tried to fix this? What did you try and what happened?"
"If you could wave a magic wand, what would the ideal state look like?"
"What would it be worth — in time saved or revenue impact — if you had that?"

[Commitment signals — 5 min]
"Who else on your team or in your network deals with this? Could you introduce me?"
"If something like what we discussed existed, what would the evaluation process look like?"

[Close]
"This has been really helpful. Is it okay if I follow up if I have more questions?"
```

## Problem Severity Scoring

Score each interview 1-5 on these dimensions:

| Dimension | 1 (weak) | 5 (strong) |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| **Frequency** | Rare, occasional | Daily or weekly |
| **Cost** | Minimal impact | High €/time cost clearly quantified |
| **Emotion** | Mild annoyance | Anger, resignation, desperation |
| **Workaround** | No workaround needed | Complex, expensive workaround in use |
| **Urgency** | "Nice to have" | "We need to solve this now" |

**Total score (max 25):**
- 20-25: Strong problem — proceed
- 15-19: Moderate problem — probe further before proceeding
- <15: Weak problem — either wrong segment or wrong problem framing

## Commitment Signal Hierarchy

Ranked by signal strength (high to low):

**Money signals:**
- Pre-order / deposit placed (strongest)
- LOI with budget allocated (B2B)
- Credit card entered for waitlist (B2C)

**Reputation signals:**
- Offered to introduce you to their team (B2B)
- Shared with friends / posted on social (B2C)
- Offered to be a reference / case study

**Time signals:**
- Agreed to a follow-up call
- Asked for a next meeting with more stakeholders (B2B)
- Signed up for update emails

**Weak / non-signals:**
- "That's interesting"
- "Keep me posted"
- LinkedIn like
- "Send me a deck"

## Common Problem Validation Failure Modes

1. **Talking to enthusiasts only** → Survivorship bias. Seek skeptics.
2. **Accepting "yes" too quickly** → Probe: "What would have to be true for you to actually use this?"
3. **Confusing curiosity with demand** → Curiosity is free. Only payment or strong commitment counts.
4. **Wrong interviewee** (B2B) → End users vs. economic buyers often give opposite signals. Talk to both.
5. **Solving a pain that exists but isn't prioritized** → Ask: "Of all the problems you face, where does this rank?"

---

## B2C vs. B2B Interview Approach Summary

| Dimension | B2C (Customer Discovery) | B2B (Stakeholder Discovery) |
|-----------|--------------------------|------------------------------|
| Who you're talking to | Individual consumer | Economic buyer + end user (interview both separately) |
| Core question | Does this problem cause enough pain to change behavior? | Can the org quantify the cost and is there a budget line? |
| Success signal | Emotional intensity, workarounds, existing spend | Cost in €/time per year, org commitment signal |
| Commitment signal | Pre-order, email signup, social share | Meeting with budget holder, LOI, pilot discussion |
| Watch out for | Friends and family validation (false positives) | Enthusiastic users whose boss won't approve the budget |
| Interview label | Customer Discovery / Mom Test | Stakeholder Discovery / Sales Discovery |
