# Bias Detection Library

Flag these biases actively during all stages of validation. When detected, surface it explicitly to the founder — don't let it pass silently.

---

## Confirmation Bias

**What it looks like:** Founder only shares interviews where people liked the idea. Dismisses negative feedback as "they just don't get it." Interprets ambiguous signals as positive.

**Detection signals:**
- All interview quotes are positive
- No mention of skeptics or doubters
- "Most people loved it" without specifics
- Immediate dismissal of kill criteria

**Intervention:**
> "You've shared mostly positive signals. Tell me about the most skeptical person you spoke to. What did they say and why did they push back?"

Force: Require 3 critical quotes before proceeding. Ask: "What would someone say who thinks this is a terrible idea?"

---

## Survivorship Bias

**What it looks like:** Only talking to people who are enthusiastic. Finding people through their network (who share their worldview). Ignoring people who said no and didn't follow up.

**Detection signals:**
- Interviewees are all friends, colleagues, or warm intros
- No diversity of opinion across interviews
- "Everyone I talked to had this problem"

**Intervention:**
> "Who have you tried to interview but couldn't get to respond? What might that tell us?"

Force: Require at least 20% of interviewees to be cold outreach strangers (B2C) or outside their professional network (B2B).

---

## Solution-First Thinking

**What it looks like:** Founder describes the product in detail in Stage 1 before validating the problem. Interviews are demos, not discovery conversations. Asks "would you use X?" instead of "tell me about your current process."

**Detection signals:**
- Founder shares product mockups in Stage 1 interviews
- Interviewee feedback is about features, not problems
- "Would you use this?" style questions (Mom Test violation)

**Intervention:**
> "The Mom Test says you should never ask about your idea in a problem discovery interview. Were you showing the product or asking about their current situation?"

Force: Return to Stage 1 with proper Mom Test interviews before proceeding to Stage 2.

## Vanity Metrics

**What it looks like:** Citing engagement metrics that don't indicate willingness to pay or behavior change. Twitter likes. LinkedIn comments. Email opens. "Virality" without purchase intent.

**Detection signals:**
- "1,000 people liked our LinkedIn post"
- "We got 500 email opens"
- "Everyone I showed it to thought it was cool"
- Traffic / impressions as proof of demand

**Intervention:**
> "Those are interesting signals, but what did people actually **do**? Did they sign up, pay, share with a colleague, ask for a demo, or commit time/money?"

Rule: Only count behavior, not opinion. The only metrics that matter are actions that cost the user something (time, money, reputation).

---

## False Positive Signals

**What it looks like:** Positive signals from people who are not the target customer. Friends and family saying yes. People being polite rather than honest. Early enthusiasts who won't represent mainstream buyers.

**Detection signals:**
- Interviewees are the founder's family, friends, or co-workers
- "Everyone I know loves the idea"
- No strangers in the validation sample
- Interviewees who can't actually buy the product

**Intervention:**
> "Would these people actually pay for this? Can they? Are they your target customer, or just people who know and like you?"

Force: For B2C — require at least 70% of interviewees to be strangers. For B2B — require interviewees to have budget authority or direct influence over purchase.

---

## Premature Scaling

**What it looks like:** Founder wants to hire, raise, or launch broadly before achieving PMF. Optimizing growth channels before retention is proven. Building team before product is validated.

**Detection signals:**
- Hiring plans before Stage 2 is complete
- Talking to investors before Stage 3
- Focusing on CAC optimization before churn is understood
- "We just need more users"

**Intervention:**
> "What's your retention/renewal rate right now? Sean Ellis score? NPS? Let's validate that people love what you have before pouring fuel on it."

Rule: Do not proceed to growth discussion until Sean Ellis ≥40% OR Stage 4 is complete.

---

## Overweighting Early Adopters

**What it looks like:** Building for the most enthusiastic 1% rather than the mainstream 90%. Features that impress tech-savvy early users but confuse normal people. Validation from early adopters taken as proof of mainstream demand.

**Detection signals:**
- All interviewees are tech-forward or startup-adjacent
- "The people who get it really love it"
- No interviews with skeptical mainstream users

**Intervention:**
> "Early adopters tolerate things mainstream customers won't. Have you validated with people who are NOT already excited about this category?"

---

## Anchoring on Initial Estimates

**What it looks like:** Sticking to an early market size estimate or pricing assumption even when new data contradicts it. "We always said the market is $1B" without updating.

**Detection signals:**
- Market size hasn't been revised despite new data
- Pricing assumptions unchanged after Van Westendorp
- Business model unchanged after multiple failed experiments

**Intervention:**
> "Your initial estimate was from [source]. We now have [new data]. Does your market size / pricing / model need to be revised?"

---

## Recency Bias

**What it looks like:** Overweighting the most recent interview or experiment result. One great conversation changes the entire assessment. One bad week of metrics causes panic.

**Detection signals:**
- "We just had a great call with [company], this is definitely going to work"
- Single data points treated as pattern confirmation

**Intervention:**
> "One data point isn't a pattern. Let's look at the aggregate across all [N] interviews / [N] days of data before drawing conclusions."
